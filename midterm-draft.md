## Title —— 项目标题、我的名字、Research Advisor: Lawted Wu

**Project Title:** The Time Poverty of the Sandwich Generation

**Presenter:** Xin Tian

**Research Advisor:** Lawted Wu

My project is a social science and interactive media project.
It studies the time poverty of the sandwich generation.
By sandwich generation, I mean people who care for older parents, care for children, and also manage work.

My artifact is an interactive task-list web demo.
Users try to finish work tasks, family tasks, care tasks, and personal rest.
But the list keeps filling up again.
The main feeling is: "I can rest after this," but "after this" never comes.

## Research Question & Hypothesis

Research Question: How does an interactive task-list demo affect users' awareness of sandwich generation time poverty and its impact on family members?

Hypothesis: I hypothesize that after using the demo, users will better understand how serious and common sandwich generation time poverty is, and see it as a structural problem.

## Background

This issue matters because time is not only personal.
Time is shaped by family, work, gender roles, care systems, and social expectations.

Many middle-aged adults are asked to be many people at once.
They are workers.
They are parents.
They are also children of aging parents.
Each role sounds reasonable by itself.
But when all roles ask for time at the same time, the person in the middle loses their own time first.

This is why I call the problem time poverty.
It is not only being busy.
It is not having enough time that truly belongs to oneself.

This problem affects more than the caregiver.
It can affect children, who may feel ignored.
It can affect older parents, who may feel guilty for needing help.

Ordinary people should care because this is not just one family's private problem.
It is connected to aging, parenting, work culture, unpaid care work, and public support.
If we notice the problem, we can start to ask better questions.
Why is care treated as only a family duty?
Why is rest always delayed first?
Why do we praise people for enduring everything, instead of asking where support is?

## Literature Review

Miller (1981) gives an early definition of the "sandwich generation."
This source helps me define the group: adults who are between older parents and their own family responsibilities.
But it does not fully show what this pressure feels like in daily life.
My project tries to make that pressure playable and visible.

Greenhaus and Beutell (1985) explain work-family conflict.
They show that work and family can conflict through time, stress, and behavior.
This helps me understand why work and family are hard to balance.
But the theory is broad, so my demo focuses on one concrete day.

Goodin and colleagues (2008) discuss discretionary time.
They argue that freedom is also about how much time a person can control.
This connects to my "self-time" bar.
My project turns this idea into an interaction.

Daminger (2019) studies the cognitive dimension of household labor.
She shows that family labor includes noticing, planning, deciding, and monitoring.
This supports my design of invisible tasks, like parent group messages and medical arrangements.
My project makes these invisible tasks visible on the screen.

Roskam, Raes, and Mikolajczak (2017) study parental burnout, and Mikolajczak and colleagues (2018) discuss its possible consequences.
These works help me think about fatigue and effects on children.
But I need to use them carefully.
My project should not blame parents.
It should show that exhaustion can grow from repeated demands.

Falkingham and colleagues (2020) study informal care across multiple generations in China.
This connects my project to the Chinese context.
It shows that multi-generation care is a real social issue.
But definitions of care differ across studies.
So my user study will focus on awareness after the demo, not on a large demographic claim.

The International Labour Organization (2018) reports the large scale of unpaid care work.
This helps me show that care is not just a small private matter.
However, large data cannot show what one family day feels like.
My contribution is to connect the large social issue to a small interactive experience.

Overall, the literature shows that sandwich generation care, work-family conflict, unpaid care, cognitive labor, and burnout are real.
What is missing is a simple way for users to feel the structure of the problem.
My demo asks users to manage the day and then see why balance is so hard.

## Research Design / Method

My artifact is a single-page interactive web demo.
It is built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.
Users can open it in a browser.

The user starts a day.
Then a task list appears.
The tasks include work, home, care, and self-time, such as replying to a boss, helping with homework, taking an older parent to a medical visit, or trying to be alone.

The demo has status bars.
The fatigue bar shows the caregiver's pressure.
The child connection bar shows the effect on the child.
The self-time bar shows whether the caregiver has time outside of social roles.

The key interaction is the "try to be alone" button.
When users click it, fatigue can drop and self-time can rise for a short moment.
But an urgent task soon appears.
This shows that rest is possible, but fragile.

For the user study, I plan to invite classmates, teachers, and other viewers to use the demo.
I may also invite people who have seen this pressure in their own family.
The user study has not been run yet.

I plan to collect three kinds of feedback.
First, the page can record simple play data, such as completed tasks, rest attempts, and interruptions.
Second, the ending screen asks open questions, such as "Who suffers most in this day?" and "Which task would you delete first?"
Third, I can use a short post-demo survey.
The survey can ask whether the demo helped users notice time poverty, family impact, and structural pressure.

For analysis, I will use simple descriptive analysis for rating questions.
For open answers, I will code repeated themes.
Possible themes include "the list never ends," "children are affected," and "the problem is structural."

## Expected Results — user study not yet run

Because the user study has not been run yet, I cannot report results.
I cannot give numbers, percentages, or final conclusions.

I expect that after using the demo, users may become more aware that sandwich generation time poverty is serious and common.
They may see that the problem is not only about one person being busy.
They may notice that the task list affects the caregiver, the child, the older parent, and the whole family.

I also expect users to notice the rest button.
When rest is interrupted, I expect some users to understand the core message:
the user did not play badly.
The system keeps adding tasks.

In open answers, I expect users may mention children, older parents, guilt, fatigue, and unfair expectations.
These are only expectations.
They are not results yet.
The real user study may confirm, weaken, or change these ideas.
